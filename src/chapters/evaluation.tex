\chapter{Evaluation}
\label{chapter:evaluation}

Data collection ran on the OpenStack cluster described in
Section~\ref{subsec:execution}. All preprocessing, model training, and
evaluation was done on WSL2 using scikit-learn~\cite{scikit-learn} 1.6.1 on
Python 3.10.12. Every accuracy figure in this chapter is a 5-fold
GroupKFold cross-validation score at the condition level: all 10 repetitions
of a condition stay in the same fold, preventing data leakage across splits.
The out-of-bag (OOB) score is reported as a secondary check.

% =============================================================================
\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

The full dataset contains 216,000 rows: 4,320 unique network conditions
measured across five implementations. Conditions are defined by seven
parameters: bandwidth (50--5,000\,Mbps), RTT (10--300\,ms), packet loss
(0--2\%), jitter (0--10\,ms), bit corruption (0--0.5\%), and two competing
traffic streams (none or 40\% of link rate for TCP and UDP independently).
Each combination was repeated 10 times to average out measurement noise.

One derived binary feature, \texttt{fc\_limited}, marks conditions where the
bandwidth-delay product exceeds 32\,MB, the initial per-stream receive window
configured for msquic. Past this point msquic's timeout rate rises sharply
(Section~\ref{subsec:fc-limited}), so the feature captures the onset of its
degradation regime:
\[
  \texttt{fc\_limited} = \mathbf{1}\!\left[
    \frac{\text{bandwidth\_mbps} \times \text{rtt\_ms}}{8000} > 32
  \right]
\]
This flag is relevant primarily in Stage~1, where it predicts msquic timeout
risk at high BDP.

A second dataset covers a systematic $20\times 20$ grid: bandwidth from 250
to 5,000\,Mbps in 250\,Mbps steps, RTT from 15 to 300\,ms in 15\,ms steps.
Each of the 400 cells was measured under four isolated conditions: no
impairments, loss\,=\,2\%, jitter\,=\,10\,ms, and corruption\,=\,0.5\%.
This dataset was collected after the main one and was not used for model
training, it exists solely to generate the heatmap visualisations in the
next section.

\section{Raw Performance}
\label{sec:raw-perf}

Before examining the recommender, it helps to see how the implementations
actually behave. Figure~\ref{fig:goodput-heatmaps} shows median goodput for
each implementation across the clean $20\times 20$ grid.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{src/img/eval/fig_goodput_heatmaps.pdf}
  \caption{Median goodput (Mbps) per implementation across the $20\times 20$
           bandwidth/RTT grid, clean condition. All panels share the same
           colour scale.}
  \label{fig:goodput-heatmaps}
\end{figure}

The differences are large. At high bandwidth and low RTT, TCP and msquic reach
the highest peaks (around 4,700\,Mbps each), with quiche close behind at roughly
3,850\,Mbps, helped by its Linux-specific GSO send path and kernel pacing via
\texttt{SO\_TXTIME}~\cite{cloudflare-quiche-gso}. quic-go peaks around
2,600\,Mbps and mvfst around 900\,Mbps, both capping out early and not gaining
any benefit from higher bandwidth.

Raw goodput in isolation does not tell the whole story. Figure~\ref{fig:bw-utilization}
shows the same data as a fraction of the configured bandwidth rather than as
an absolute throughput number.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{src/img/eval/fig_bw_utilization.pdf}
  \caption{Bandwidth utilisation (goodput / configured bandwidth, \%) for each
           implementation in clean conditions. Darker cells indicate higher
           fraction of line-rate achieved.}
  \label{fig:bw-utilization}
\end{figure}

At low RTT, TCP and msquic nearly saturate the link. As RTT grows, every
implementation degrades but at very different rates. At 5,000\,Mbps and
300\,ms RTT (BDP $\approx 187$\,MB), quiche, quic-go, and mvfst all fall to
roughly 1--2\% utilisation, unable to fill the pipe. msquic does better at 62\%,
but is still far from line-rate. The link is not congested, which means the
implementations are the bottleneck. This spread is one of the main things
the recommender needs to capture.

Figure~\ref{fig:winner-map-raw} shows which implementation has the highest
raw goodput in each cell under clean conditions. TCP and msquic win most of
the grid. quiche dominates at high BDP where its GSO-assisted send path
compensates for its higher-latency userspace stack.

msquic degrades the most under loss in the low-BW, low-RTT region, the same

\section{Profile Label Distributions}
\label{sec:label-dist}

Raw performance data shows that implementations differ, but choosing the right
one depends on what the deployment cares about. The five profiles defined in
Section~\ref{subsec:profiles} assign a composite score per implementation per
condition using different weightings of goodput, latency, resource usage, and
loss recovery. The winner under each profile becomes the training label for
Stage~2.

Figure~\ref{fig:winner-distribution} and Table~\ref{tab:winner-dist} show
how often each implementation wins per profile.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{src/img/eval/fig_winner_distribution.pdf}
  \caption{Fraction of the 4,320 conditions won by each implementation under
           each profile's composite scoring. mvfst and quic-go together win at
           most 1.1\% of conditions in any profile.}
  \label{fig:winner-distribution}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Per-profile winner distribution: fraction of the 4,320 conditions
           in which each implementation receives the highest composite score.}
  \label{tab:winner-dist}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrrrr}
    \hline
    \textbf{Profile} & \textbf{quiche} & \textbf{tcp} & \textbf{msquic}
                     & \textbf{mvfst}  & \textbf{quic-go} \\
    \hline
    datacenter         & 90.2\% &  2.0\% &  7.8\% & 0.0\% & 0.0\% \\
    lightweight        &  0.4\% & 99.4\% &  0.1\% & 0.0\% & 0.0\% \\
    mobile\_lossy      & 66.3\% & 28.1\% &  5.1\% & 0.4\% & 0.1\% \\

The profiles produce very different answers. quiche wins 90.2\% of datacenter
conditions because that profile weights goodput at 0.80 and quiche's GSO
send path is the fastest on that metric. TCP wins 99.4\% of lightweight
conditions because that profile weights server CPU at 0.30 and TCP's
kernel-space stack consumes several times less CPU than quiche (a median of
roughly seven times less server CPU across clean runs in this dataset). The latency-sensitive and deep-space profiles produce near-ties
between quiche and TCP (39.7\%/49.8\% and 46.2\%/47.8\% respectively),
reflecting conditions where quiche's throughput advantage trades off against
TCP's lower overhead. mvfst and quic-go never win enough to matter in any
profile.

implementation wins per (bandwidth, RTT) cell for each profile.

\begin{figure}[htbp]

The datacenter map is mostly quiche at high BDP and a mix at low BDP. The

changes the recommendation.

tail RTT in a way that happens to prefer the same candidate across most cells.

\section{Model Accuracy}
\label{sec:model-accuracy}

\subsection{Stage 1: timeout classifiers}
\label{subsec:stage1-acc}

Stage~1 trains one binary Random Forest per implementation to predict whether
a given set of conditions will cause a timeout. Table~\ref{tab:stage1-acc}
reports the results.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Stage~1 timeout classifier accuracy per implementation
           (5-fold GroupKFold CV, with OOB as a secondary check).}
  \label{tab:stage1-acc}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrr}
    \hline
    \textbf{Implementation} & \textbf{CV accuracy} & \textbf{CV std} & \textbf{OOB} \\
    \hline
    quic-go  & 100.00\% & 0.00\%  & 100.00\% \\
    tcp      &  99.998\% & 0.005\% &  99.977\% \\
    quiche   &   99.49\% & 0.09\%  &   98.05\% \\
    mvfst    &   96.43\% & 0.34\%  &   92.28\% \\
    msquic   &   85.67\% & 0.59\%  &   83.31\% \\
    \hline
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

quic-go and TCP are trivial cases: with zero and one timeout across 43,200
runs respectively, predicting "no timeout" for every row achieves

decision boundary: even within the \texttt{fc\_limited} regime, some
high-RTT conditions complete successfully and some do not, and no single
feature cleanly separates them.

Datacenter is the opposite case: a median gap of 0.507 means quiche
outscores the alternatives by a large margin in most conditions, and 94.38\%
accuracy follows naturally.

\section{Feature Importance}
\label{sec:feature-importance}

Figure~\ref{fig:feature-importance} shows permutation importance for the
Stage~2 classifiers. Permutation importance is used rather than mean
decrease in impurity (MDI) because it is computed on held-out data and does
not inflate importance for high-cardinality continuous features~\cite{strobl2007bias}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{src/img/eval/fig_feature_importance.pdf}
  \caption{Permutation importance for the Stage~2 ranker, grouped by profile.
           Each group covers the eight input features. Error bars show standard
           deviation across permutation runs.}
  \label{fig:feature-importance}
\end{figure}

Four things stand out.

\textbf{Loss is the top feature for three profiles.} For datacenter (0.143),
mobile\_lossy (0.293), and deep\_space (0.292), packet loss has the highest
permutation score. The goodput gap between implementations is widest under
loss, because their congestion-control behaviours diverge the most when the
network is lossy.

\textbf{RTT leads for latency-sensitive.} The profile's 0.30 weight on tail
RTT shifts dominance to rtt\_ms (0.262). Jitter, which is negligible in every
other profile, rises to 0.139 here, the only context where per-packet delay
variation carries meaningful discriminative power.

\textbf{Lightweight is flat.} The maximum permutation score in the lightweight
profile is 0.011. Since TCP wins 99.4\% of labels, almost any feature is
sufficient to produce a correct prediction and none stands out. MDI values are
substantially higher here, a known artefact of MDI overestimating importance
in near-single-class problems~\cite{strobl2007bias}.

\textbf{\texttt{fc\_limited} has consistently low Stage~2 importance.} Across
all five profiles, \texttt{fc\_limited} sits at the bottom or near the bottom
(range: 0.0002 to 0.025). Its role is in Stage~1, where it strongly predicts
msquic timeout risk at high BDP. Once timeouts are handled, it adds little
signal to the ranking decision.

\section{Live Prober Validation}
\label{sec:prober-validation}

The prober was run inside the same Docker topology used for data collection:
a three-container setup with a probe server, a network simulator applying
\texttt{tc netem} conditions, and a probe client. Each run used
\texttt{--rounds~3} (three measurement iterations, results averaged) and the
default 10\% timeout-elimination threshold. Bandwidth was supplied directly
via \texttt{--bw} rather than measured by iperf3, to avoid saturating the
netem buffer in the Docker Desktop WSL2 environment at low configured rates.

Table~\ref{tab:prober-validation} summarises the five static profile tests.
The prober reproduced configured RTT within 1\,ms in every case. Measured loss
ran above the configured value in the mobile\_lossy and deep\_space scenarios.
The prober estimates loss from a small ICMP ping sample, and a ping is counted
as lost if either its request or its reply is dropped, so the expected estimate
is the round-trip loss $1-(1-p)^2$ (roughly $2p$) rather than the one-way
configured $p$. The match is only approximate: with a few tens of probes per
run the estimate is noisy, and at high RTT with jitter some replies arrive after
the ping timeout and add to the count. This is why deep\_space (configured 3\%,
measured 8.3\%) overshoots even the round-trip value while mobile\_lossy
(configured 2\%, measured 3.3\%) sits just below it.

The results break into three groups.

\textbf{Unstable recommendations (datacenter, lightweight).} Both came back
below the 60\% confidence threshold. At zero loss and low RTT, the scoring
gap between the top candidates is narrow (consistent with the ambiguity
data in Section~\ref{sec:model-accuracy}). The datacenter RTT of 10.3\,ms
is also just below the training grid minimum of 15\,ms, so the model is
mildly extrapolating. The prober correctly signals uncertainty rather than
committing to a wrong recommendation.

\textbf{Clear outcomes (latency-sensitive, deep\_space).} The
latency-sensitive result (msquic, 61.1\%) is above the threshold and STABLE.
Deep space is the clearest result in the whole test: three QUIC implementations
eliminated by Stage~1, TCP at 100\% confidence. This makes sense: at 5\,Mbps
and 300\,ms with elevated loss, quiche and msquic time out too often to be
reliable, and mvfst also fails its Stage~1 gate. TCP's stable kernel stack is
the only sensible choice.

\textbf{Stage~1 overrides Stage~2 (mobile\_lossy).} This is the most
interesting case, because the two stages pull in opposite directions. On raw
performance quiche is the strongest candidate, and Stage~2's ranking puts it
first. But the measured loss averaged 3.3\%, above the 2\% maximum seen during
training, and Stage~1 flags quiche as likely to time out (19.6\%, over the 10\%
threshold), alongside msquic (23.8\%). Since an eliminated implementation
cannot be recommended, the prober drops quiche and returns TCP at 95.9\%
confidence, while noting explicitly that quiche was the top performer but was
ruled out on reliability. This is what the two-stage design is for: Stage~1
guards against an implementation that looks fast on average yet fails outright
in a meaningful fraction of runs, and the trade-off is reported rather than
hidden.

\subsection{Parameter sweeps}
\label{subsec:sweeps}

The static tests above cover fixed operating points. The sweep experiments
show how behaviour changes continuously as a single parameter varies.
Figure~\ref{fig:sweep-loss} shows the loss sweep: BW\,=\,100\,Mbps,
RTT\,=\,50\,ms, profile\,=\,mobile\_lossy, loss from 0 to 10\%.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering

  \caption{Loss sweep (BW\,=\,100\,Mbps, RTT\,=\,50\,ms, profile\,=\,mobile\_lossy).

UNSTABLE. Meanwhile, msquic is close behind and the two are nearly tied. At 2\% loss,
msquic's timeout probability crosses 22.7\% and Stage~1 eliminates it. With
msquic gone, quiche's confidence jumps to 80.6\% and the recommendation
becomes STABLE. At 5\% and 10\% loss the confidence rises further to 96--97\%
as the field narrows further. The stability gate does exactly what it should:
low confidence when implementations are equivalent, high confidence once the
field is pruned.

At 10\,ms RTT, msquic wins with 71.5\% confidence and the result is STABLE.
Above that, confidence drops and results become UNSTABLE, which means that this
profile is in a region of high label ambiguity (quiche and TCP are close in
the scoring) and three rounds of probing is not enough to resolve close calls.
At 60\,ms and above, the recommendation shifts to TCP. At 300\,ms, mvfst's
timeout probability reaches 73.7\% and Stage~1 eliminates it. The recommendation
flip from msquic to TCP at high RTT is consistent with the bandwidth
utilisation data in Section~\ref{sec:raw-perf}: msquic's performance
degrades at high BDP while TCP maintains better utilisation, and the
latency-sensitive profile's tail RTT weight amplifies this difference
into a clear preference.
